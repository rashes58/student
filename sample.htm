ABSTRACT

 

Colorectal cancer is a deadly disease which affects hundreds and thousands of individuals each year. There is a need to understand the underlying genetic mechanisms involved in order to develop effective methods for the diagnosis and treatment, and, eventually, cure colorectal cancer. The purpose of this study was to build a transcriptome profile for CRC and identify the key regulatory genes involved. This study also aimed at finding the molecular signatures using the genes identified and understand the molecular mechanisms that played a role in linking the genotype to phenotype. Significant genes were identified by mapping datasets to a reference genome and based on the counts table generated, DESeq2 was used for gene analysis. Volcano plots exhibit the expression of up-regulated and downregulated genes. 469 up-regulated genes and 162 downregulated genes were identified. Clustering analysis was done to find networks and top ten significant genes were used to find molecular signatures. The shortest paths among the target (seed) and source (significant genes) were found. Variant calling was done where somatic and germline variants were identified as well as variants which were affected by loss of heterozygosity. Variants and genes affected by these variants were found and they were annotated according to general human genetic and cancer-specific databases.

 

 

Introduction

Cancer is a deadly disease which is on the rise. Colorectal cancer (CRC) is that which occurs in the colon and the rectum. Hundreds of thousands of individuals are diagnosed each year and due to this it is ranked high under basic cancers. Compared to males, females are more commonly affected and this makes it second common cancer in women and third in men (Stewart and Kleihues 2003). In the Western countries, its prevalence is much higher than that in India. In terms of mortality causes, it is ranked seventh in India. Recent study conducted showed that as many as 28,000 cases were diagnosed with mortality of around 21,000. It is estimated that there could be approximately 50,000 active cases in India as of now. Normally CRC affects individuals over the age of 50 however there has been an increase in the number of cases in the younger population. About 32% of the individuals are younger than 45.

A miniscule fraction of CRCs is caused due to mutations in the inherited genes. APC is involved in tumor suppression and plays a vital role in keeping cell growth under check. Irregularities in genes involved in repair, such as PMS2, MLH1, MLH2 AND MLH6, could lead to oversight while regulating growth related genes and may prove to be a key factor in cancer formation (Lynch and de la Chapelle 2003). Similarly, STK11, which encodes for serine, plays a role in suppression. MUTYH manages the analysis of cell and variation in this gene can cause rampant, unchecked division.

Three notable pathways affect play a role in CRC (Vogelstein and Kinzler 2004). The first pathway is Chromosomal Instability (CIN). This is a traditional pathway and is successful in explaining large number of cancers (Grady and Carethers 2008). It describes the uncertainties in chromosomes and their number which eventually leads to tumor and loss of one copy of a gene. Working principle covers the revision in chromosome division and irregulation in telomere. This in turn affects the working capacity of genes such as APC, KRAS, PI3K and TP53. Variation in TP53 causes uninterrupted entry in cell cycle (Pino and Chung 2010). Microsatellite instability pathway comes into play when DNA repair is no longer effective and this causes overexpression of phenotype. DNA repair ability is severely compromised and variations end up compiling which eventually affects coding and non-coding regions in microsatellites. The reading frame gets affected and tumors form. These are primarily diploid. Genes affected are MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS1 and PMS2 (Boland and Goel 2010). Epigenetic instability plays a vital role in CpG islands. CIMP tumors cause excessive methylation and this causes silencing and eventually loss of protein expression.

Genetics and epigenetics together help in advancement of CRC and there is increased methylation compared to variations (Lao and Grady 2011). Example of this is BRAF mutations along with microsatellite instability CIMP tumors (Weisenberger et al. 2006).

Gene expression profiling is the comparison between the expression in tumor and normal tissue samples. This is essential as it provides valuable information and aids in prognosis (Bertucci et al. 2004). Advancements have been done in these assays and advantages for patients as well as society have been highlighted. However, there isnt enough proof to validate possible benefits to chemotherapy. Further studies and exploration is needed to validate them clinically.

Review of Literature

Molecular based diagnostic methods

Colorectal Cancer is a deadly disease and the cause of countless deaths. The microbial organisms in the gut of an individual play a role in forming carcinogenic colonies by following a pathway linked to inflammation (Mrmol et al. 2017). Recent studies have shown that screening on a regular basis can help detect any traces of CRC and these growths can be removed before they turn life threatening (Colorectal Cancer | Early Detection and Screening | CancerCare n.d.). One of the methods for detecting mutations in CRC is allele-specific PCR. This has the ability to detect APS and KRAS mutations similar to the Sanger sequencing method. Sanger sequencing focuses on the area of interest and narrows down the nucleotide sequences. It can even detect novel changes but since the run time and cost is high, this method is not considered for routine checks. Allele- specific PCR amplifies the mutational sequence by using primers. This increases speed and accuracy and is therefore preferred over Sanger sequencing (Sundstrm et al. 2010). Real-time PCR makes use of a fluorogenic probe which is exclusive to the target sequence. Change in the fluorescent signals is used to monitor target DNA molecules. This takes less time than Sanger sequencing and allele-specific PCR as the turnover time is less (Legolvan, Taliano, and Resnick 2012).

B. Differential Gene Expression of colorectal cancer genes

Differential gene expression was done using Galaxy software and various tools available within. Quality control is a necessary step before proceeding with mapping and MultiQC, a tool to create a single report visualising output from multiple tools across many samples was used.(Ewels et al. 2016) In a study done by Alexander Dobin and Thomas R. Gingerasm (2015) it was found that the STAR software package performed the task of mapping with high speed and accuracy. It was also found that STAR was useful for generating files which could be used for downstream analysis such as differential gene expression, gene expression quantification, novel isoform reconstruction, etc. For further analysis, it was important to know the number of reads that were mapped to each of the genomic features, i.e., to each gene or exon. This process known as summarisation is required for genomic analysis. Data obtained from STAR was analysed using FeatureCounts developed by Yang Liao ,Gordon Smyth and Wei Shi (2014). They proposed that featureCounts used highly effective feature blocking technique and chromosome hashing techniques. It was proved to be much faster that the already existing techniques and utilised far comparatively lesser computer memory. It works with single ended as well as pair ended reads and provides a range of options for various sequencing options. Crucial step in analysis of the data obtained from RNA-seq, count data, is the detection of the differentially expressed genes. A question arises regarding the quantification and interference of systematic changes between provided conditions as compared to that within condition variability. Theis was answered by M. I. Love, W. Huber, S. Anders when they came up with the DESeq2 package which provide methods for testing the estimates of dispersion, generalised linear models and logarithmic fold change to in corporate the data driven prior distributions (Love, Anders, and Huber 2016). This paper focused on the changes made to DESeq2 from its earlier version DESeq. The new DESeq2 package uses weighted quantile function of Hmisc package instead of the earlier methods of matching the empirical quantile to the normal distribution quantile for the calculation of beta prior variance (October 2014). The paper published in 2014 mentioned that DESeq used log fold shrinkage to only applied interaction terms however in a change implemented in 2015, DESeq2 was programmed to turn off the log fold shrinkage for all terms if interaction terms were present.

Volcano plots are graphical tools which are used for analysis of levels of mRNA expression. This data is mainly obtained via microarray technology (biology and 2012 2011; Cui and Churchill 2003; Wolfinger et al. 2001). Volcano plots are a type of scatter plots where each point on the plot represents a gene or a probe set. The x-coordinate is the (log) fold-change (FC) and the y-coordinate is the t-statistic or − log 10 of the p-value from a t-test. Volcano plots are preferred for microarray analysis due to its ability to simultaneously display two, correlated pieces of information, i.e., fold change and t-statistics. Major advantage of volcano plot is that it addresses joint gene filtering (biology and 2012 2011; Zhang and Cao 2009) where both log FC and t- statistic is used to measure differential expressions.

C. Molecular Signatures

Signalling pathways represented as mechanistic models lay out a bridge from the variations in phenotype to the variations in the genotype. This is done at the scale of gene integrity or activity. These bridges are built over graphs and represent the knowledge of compound functional relationships among the proteins present within the cells as described in major repositories namely Reactome, KEGG, or other more specialised and complex such as Disease Maps. They provide a theoretical framework for analysis of genomic variation or gene expression and their effect over phenotypic responses like cell death and proliferation and the downstream processes involved which are needed for understanding drug response and progression of the disease. These mechanistic models have given insight into various disease mechanisms behind rare diseases, diabetes, cancers including neuroblastoma and glioblastoma, obesity.

PathLinker is a signalling pathway reconstruction method used for constructing protein interactions and regulatory networks. It mainly connects the receptors in a given query to the transcription factors. Its main use is to connect sources to any given targets in an interaction network. The working algorithm of PathLinker is computation of the k-shortest and simplest paths in a given network from a particular source to a target. It incorporates Yens algorithm which allows effective computation of k values. The output is a sub-network composed of the k shortest paths.

D. Variant Calling

The process of identifying variants from sequence datasets is known as variant calling. It is done by aligning sequence to the reference genome and creating BAM files. When analysing germline variant calling, the reference genome for the species of interest is standard. This aids in the identification of genotypes. Since most of the genomes are diploid, at any given locus, all reads either have same base which indicates homozygosity or half of these reads will have one base and the other half have another base thus indicating heterozygosity. However, in somatic variant calling, a related tissue from the same individual is taken as the reference. Mosaicism can be expected to between cells.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Methods and Materials

RNA Sequence Data

The Fastq files for RNA sequence analysis were obtained online from GEO (Gene Expression Omnibus). 4 diseased and 4 adjacent controls for colorectal cancer were taken from the GEO series GSE50760. All the datasets obtained were pair ended.

Differential Gene Expression of RNA Sequence Data

In this part differentially expressed genes were detected. The following is the procedure used to detect the DE genes and find out the genes of significance and in turn generate a volcano plot for visualisation.

       The datasets were uploaded online and analysed using the GALAXY tool.

       FastQC was used to produce a report stating the quality of the sequences and MultiQC (Ewels et al. 2016) was used to combine the reports generated by FastQC.

       Cutadapt (Martin 2011) was used to further improve the quality by trimming.

       Gene annotation file was obtained from the UCSC Main server with the ncbiRefSeq form of annotation. RNA Star (Dobin et al. 2013) was used to align the RNA-seq reads to the reference genome. The reference genome used was hg38.

       Featurecounts was used on the obtained report to count the number of genes per annotated gene.

       DESeq2 (Love, Anders, and Huber 2016) was used to normalise and analyse the obtained count data and produce a combined table.

       In order to extract the genes with a significant change in gene expression, i.e., adjusted p-value below 0.05, filter data on any column using simple expression function was used.

       Further the genes with absolute fold change (FC) > 2 were selected.

       The obtained file was further annotated to find the gene names, functions and the location of the gene within the genome.

       DeSeq2 file was used to generate the volcano plot for visualisation of upregulated and down regulated genes.

       DeSeq2 file was used to segregate the up regulated and the down regulated genes according to its expression level.

       The gene IDs of the regulated genes were submitted to an ID conversion and gene set analysis tool (SynGo) in order to find the entrez gene number, name and function of the gene, taxid and symbol, alias and HGNC.

 

 

Obtaining Molecular Signatures

Clustering was done using 2 types of analysis; clustering analysis and shortest path analysis. This was done using the software Cytoscape. Packages such as GeneMania, MCODE and PathLinker were installed.

       The genes were separated as up-regulated and down-regulated. Gene Symbols of the upregulated genes were selected and uploaded to GeneMania, thereby creating a network of all the upregulated genes.

       MCODE was used to analyse the network formed and seed nodes for the network were isolated.

       The first ten most upregulated genes were taken and its position in the network was noted.

       PathLinker was used for the ten selected genes and the seed genes and a cluster was generated showing shortest path.

       The same was done for downregulated genes and another cluster was obtained.

Variant Calling

Direct comparison of data is needed between data obtained from normal and tumor tissue samples in order to differentiate between these two types of variants. This process was completed using Galaxy. Following is the process used for variant calling.

       The same four diseased datasets and four controls were used. Quality control was done using FastQC and MultiQC.

       Trimmomatic was used to trim and filter the normal tissue reads as well as tumor reads.

       The reads from normal tissue sample and tumor samples were mapped to reference genome using Map with BWA-MEM tool. Reference genome used here is hg19.

       Filter BAM datasets on a variety of attribute tool was used to produce BAM dataset with reads which were successfully mapped to the reference genome.

       RmDup was used to remove duplicate reads from normal as well as tumor datasets.

       BamLeftAlign was used to left-align reads around indels. Read mapping qualities were recalibrated using CalMD. Filter BAM datasets on a variety of attributes function was used to remove reads where mapping quality was set to 255 by CalMD.

       VarScan somatic was used for classifying variants into germline, somatic and LOH event variants.

       Variant and gene annotation files were uploaded onto galaxy namely variant annotations from Cancer Hotspots, the Cancer Biomarkers database of the Cancer Genome Interpreter (CGI) project, the CIViC database, variant annotations from dbSNP and lists of genes annotated with the keywords proto-oncogene or tumor suppressor at UniProt.

       Genomic annotations were added to dataset of variants with SnpEff. Clinical and genetic evidence-based annotations were added using GEMINI.

       GEMINI annotate was used to add annotations to the variants in an already loaded GEMINI database. It was also used to add information from dbSNP, cancer hotspots and Cancer Genome Interpreter (CGI) and links to the CIViC database.

       GEMINI query was used for querying database for somatic variants and for generating reports of genes affected by variants.

       Information about UniProt cancer genes, CGI biomarkers and CIViC was further added to the report and columns were arranged accordingly.

Discussion

This study was to done to build a transcriptome profile for CRC and identify the key regulatory genes involved. Transcriptome contains the entire messenger RNA (mRNA) in an individual. It contains the genetic code that is transcribed into RNA molecules. In this study eight datasets were analyzed, four tumor tissue samples and four adjacent normal unaffected tissue samples. Datasets were checked for quality and trimmed accordingly. RNA STAR was used the map datasets onto a reference genome. Reference genome used in this study was hg38. Upon mapping, STAR produced a BAM file containing information about the sequence quality and location of reads on the chromosome and the mapping quality. Upon quality check, MultiQC report showed that more than 80% of the reads were uniquely aligned to the reference genome. These were the finding for all the datasets taken. These results were viewed using Integrative Genomics Viewer (IGV, Robinson et al. 2011). A counts table was generated with the number of fragments mapped to a particular gene. This table was used to as an input for DESeq2 which is a tool for sorting RNA sequence data and running differential gene analysis. Two factors were taken: tumor and normal. Three main results were generated as a result of DESeq2. The first was a table which contained the normalised counts. The second was a graphical summary containing five components. The first was a PCA plot generated on the account of normalised counts, the next was a heatmap highlighting the similarities and dissimilarities between the samples. The third and fourth were dispersion estimates and a histogram of p values for genes in comparison. The fifth and the main component was the MA plot. Working theory for the plot is that most genes do not experience a change in their expression and should be plotted at 0 on the y-axis because log (1) is 0. The genes which passed a significance threshold value of 0.1 (p- value < 0.1) were marked in red. Those genes lying above M=0 are the up-regulated genes and those below are the downregulated genes. The last result generated by DESeq2 was a file which contained following values for every gene: identification, Wald statistic, fold change and standard error in log2, p-value and the adjusted p-value. Genes with a significant change (adjusted p-value < 0.05) were extracted. 631 genes were found to be significantly expressed. In order to check for the up and downregulated, a volcano plot was plotted. DESeq2 file containing values for each gene was used. Significant threshold was kept at 0.05. The results showed a plot with 469 up-regulated genes and 162 downregulated genes. SLC35D3, STC2, COMP, CYP2B6, BOK-AS1, FOXQ1, CEL, EMSLR and CCAT1 are the top ten upregulated genes. AK1, KRTAP13-2, ZNF492, B3GALT1, RETNLB, MT1M, B3GALT5-AS1, B3GNT7, WFDC2 and HIGD1A are the top ten downregulated genes. Genes were sorted based on their regulation and they were used to produce density-based clusters. Two networks were built: a network comprising of up-regulated genes and a network comprising of down regulated genes. MCODE analysis produced a list of seeds. Seeds are the highly connected genes in a cluster. Significant genes affect the seed and as a result the genes interacting with the seed are affected as well. From the cluster of up-regulated genes 11 seeds were identified. PathLinker was used to calculate the shortest path. PathLinker needs sources (significant genes) and targets (seeds) to calculate the path. The 11 seed genes were treated as targets and the top 10 up-regulated genes were used to create molecular signature highlighting the shortest path between the seeds and genes. a total of 50 shortest possible paths were generated. 13 paths were identified with a path score of 1 and the rest had a path score of 2. The path ranked first was between ADAMTS2 (significant gene) and IGFBP5 (seed). Similar method was followed for downregulated genes. From the cluster of downregulated genes, 5 seeds were identified. Top ten downregulated genes were used to create molecular signatures. This resulted in the creation of 50 shortest paths. 8 paths were identified with a path score of 1 and the rest had a path score 2. The shortest path which was ranked first was between LGALS3 (significant gene) and MT1HL1 (seed). Variant calling is the process of identifying variants from the sequence datasets. In this study, somatic and germline variants were identified from tumor and normal sample datasets. Datasets were checked for quality and trimmed and mapped to reference genome. VarScan somatic was used to determine the variant present in tumor and normal pair and classify variants into germline, somatic and LOH variants. GEMINI was used to annotate variants and extract information from VarScan results. Annotations from various cancer databases were added to GEMINI in order to create a detailed report. Essentially at this point a report with somatic status, cancer hotspot information, links for CIVic sites and CGI data is created. In dataset SRR975569, total number of variants identified were 54915 (germline: 50416, somatic: 2772, LOH: 1712). In dataset SRR975570, total number of variants identified were 34145 (germline: 30539, somatic: 2331, LOH: 1264). In dataset SRR975571, total number of variants identified were 47640 (germline: 45729, somatic: 1713, LOH: 197). In dataset SRR975572, total number of variants identified were 48991 (germline: 44514, somatic: 2784, LOH: 1677). However, a detailed report is further created where only somatic variations are considered. Somatic variants with p-value < 0.05 were considered. Tables provided in the study mention the gene affected by the variant and other important features such as nature of gene (tumor suppressor gene or oncogene), chromosome number and entrez number. In dataset SRR975569, 72 genes affected by somatic variants were identified; in SRR975570, 52 genes were identified; in SRR975571, 58 genes were identified and in SRR975572, 58 genes, which were affected by somatic variants, were identified.

 

 

 

 

 

Conclusion

This study was to done to build a transcriptome profile for CRC and identify the key regulatory genes involved. Eight mRNA datasets were analyzed and RNA sequencing was done in order to get the differentially expressed genes. When data was mapped to a reference genome after quality checks and appropriate trimming, about 80% of the reads were mapped uniquely. Counts table was generated for the purpose of finding out the number of fragments mapped and subsequently obtain the differentially obtained genes. DESeq2 was used to generate a file with information needed to plot a volcano plot. Based on this plot, up-regulated and down regulated genes were visualized. 469 up-regulated genes and 162 downregulated genes were obtained. These genes served as a basis for creating and visualizing molecular signatures and eventually finding the shortest path between significant genes and the seed genes. 50 shortest paths were obtained for up-regulated as well as downregulated clusters. Variant calling was performed with tumor and normal data pairs. Different variations (somatic, germline and LOH event mutations) were found in every dataset. On finer refinement, tables containing only somatic variations which passed p-value < 0.05 were allowed and the genes affected by these variations were highlighted
